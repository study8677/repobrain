# Non-Negotiable Marketing Communication Standard

Status: Mandatory for all CorpFlowAI prospect-facing and client-facing work  
Owner: Commercial / Brand / Product Delivery  
Applies to: Websites, landing pages, social posts, emails, proposals, decks, onboarding content, help articles, client updates, sales scripts, video scripts, visual briefs, and AI-generated copy.

## 1. Executive rule

CorpFlowAI does not choose between text, video, graphics, or design polish. Every external communication must combine:

1. A fast attention layer.
2. A scannable comprehension layer.
3. A proof-rich validation layer.
4. A beautiful, disciplined presentation layer.
5. A clear next action.

The standard is not “make it pretty.” The standard is: make it easier to trust, easier to understand, easier to act on, and harder to ignore.

## 2. Core research translation

The research finding is not that people stopped reading. The finding is that people reserve deep reading for moments that matter and expect digital communication to be visual, scannable, useful, and immediately credible.

Therefore:

- Video and visuals win attention.
- Scannable text wins comprehension.
- Proof, comparison, and implementation detail win decisions.
- Consistency across website, sales, onboarding, and support wins trust.
- Aesthetic quality increases perceived competence only when it supports clarity.

## 3. Mandatory communication model

Every deliverable must answer these five questions visibly:

1. Who is this for?
2. What problem are we solving?
3. Why should the prospect believe us?
4. What should they do next?
5. What asset helps them validate the claim if they are not ready to act?

No external-facing copy is complete unless it contains a hook, proof, and depth path.

## 4. The Hook / Proof / Depth doctrine

### Hook

The hook earns attention. It may be a headline, hero statement, short video opening, visual contrast, carousel opening frame, email subject, or first sentence.

A valid hook must be specific, commercially relevant, and tied to a real buyer problem.

Invalid hook patterns:

- Generic hype.
- Empty urgency.
- “Revolutionary” language without evidence.
- Visual drama that does not clarify the offer.

### Proof

Proof earns trust. Proof may be a metric, case example, testimonial, screenshot, workflow evidence, before/after comparison, documented process, public source, client result, or operational artifact.

Every important claim must be paired with proof or labeled as a hypothesis.

### Depth

Depth earns conversion. Depth may be a landing page, FAQ, comparison table, implementation plan, objection handling block, pricing explanation, technical note, case study, onboarding guide, or proposal section.

Every attention asset should point to a validation asset.

## 5. Required dual-asset pattern

Whenever an AI agent produces an attention asset, it must also produce or reference a validation asset.

Examples:

- Social post -> landing page section or FAQ.
- Short video script -> proof block and CTA page.
- Hero section -> below-the-fold proof section.
- Email outreach -> objection response and follow-up asset.
- Proposal intro -> implementation timeline and risk-reduction proof.
- Onboarding message -> searchable guide or checklist.

Agents may not stop at a hook unless the task explicitly asks for brainstorming only.

## 6. Aesthetic standard

Aesthetic quality is mandatory because it signals operational quality. However, beauty may never weaken clarity.

All designs and visual briefs must prioritize:

- Strong hierarchy.
- Clean spacing.
- High contrast.
- Clear section logic.
- Minimal visual noise.
- Meaningful imagery.
- Screenshots, diagrams, or product visuals over generic stock imagery.
- Premium restraint over decorative excess.

The visual layer must make the message easier to understand.

## 7. Copy standard

All copy must be:

- Plain-spoken.
- Buyer-specific.
- Scannable.
- Evidence-aware.
- Commercially direct.
- Free of unsupported hype.
- Structured around the buyer’s situation, not CorpFlowAI’s internal excitement.

Preferred writing pattern:

1. Lead with the conclusion.
2. State the buyer problem.
3. Explain the operational impact.
4. Provide proof or a concrete mechanism.
5. Offer one next step.

## 8. Trust standard

Trust is damaged when the website, sales copy, proposals, onboarding, and client management content say different things.

Agents must reuse approved claims, approved positioning, approved offer language, and approved proof wherever available.

If an agent cannot find proof, it must say so and produce a lower-risk version of the claim.

## 9. Decision-stage rules

### Awareness

Use video, visuals, short posts, sharp headlines, and high-contrast ideas. Keep the message simple. Always route to a validation asset.

### Consideration

Use scannable pages, FAQs, comparisons, role-specific explanations, objection handling, and customer stories.

### Conversion

Use specific proof, risk reduction, implementation clarity, pricing logic, and a single clear next step.

### Onboarding

Use paired video walkthroughs and searchable written guides.

### Retention and expansion

Use success checklists, use-case prompts, adoption reports, improvement recommendations, and client-visible progress updates.

## 10. Non-negotiable final test

Before delivery, every external communication must pass this test:

- Would a skeptical buyer understand the value in under 10 seconds?
- Would a serious evaluator find enough proof to continue?
- Would an internal stakeholder be able to forward it without embarrassment?
- Does it look like it came from a competent, premium, detail-oriented company?
- Is the next step obvious?

If any answer is no, the deliverable is not ready.
